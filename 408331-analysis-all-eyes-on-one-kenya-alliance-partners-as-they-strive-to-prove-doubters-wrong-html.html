Analysis: All Eyes on One Kenya Alliance Partners as They Strive to Prove Doubters Wrong

The formation of political alliances is once again the in thing in Kenya as the country heads to another General Election next year.

It is that season where meetings between leaders trigger all manner of speculation about the political direction they want to take.

While many alliances are in the offing, one has already been born and hit the ground running.

The One Kenya Alliance (OKA) brings together Wiper leader Kalonzo Musyoka, Amani’s Musalia Mudavadi, Gideon Moi of KANU, and Moses Wetang’ula of Ford Kenya.

The alliance, which is said to have the blessings of President Uhuru Kenyatta, touts itself as the most viable formation to form the next government.

Its leaders have sought to front the alliance as an alternative to former Prime Minister Raila Odinga of the Orange Democratic Movement (ODM) and Deputy President William Ruto, who is set to vie for the presidency on a United Democratic Alliance (UDA) ticket.

“We stand here to tell Kenyans that if you thought there was no alternative, please start putting it in your mind that there is an alternative of hope in this country,” Mudavadi declared on March 25, when it was formally unveiled in Nairobi.

The question that persists, however, is whether the alliance will stand the test of time and romp to the State House or it will go the way of other previous similar formations that emerged on the scene with a lot of promise only to die even before the Election Day.

There was, for instance, the G7 Alliance that brought together political parties such as The National Alliance of President Kenyatta, Eugene Wamalwa’s New Ford- Kenya, Kalonzo’s Wiper, Alliance Party of Kenya of Kiraitu Murungi, and Raphael Tuju’s Party of Action.

They aimed to form a giant political party, Alliance National Coalition (ANC), which they intended to use to vie for power in the 2013 General Election.

However, the envisaged party never saw the light of day as political realignments saw Kenyatta join forces with United Republican Party (URP)’s William Ruto to form Jubilee Alliance.

On the other hand, Kalonzo shifted base and linked up with ODM's Raila to form the Coalition for Reforms and Democracy (Cord).

The two outfits went into the election as favourites but in the end, Jubilee got the better of Cord in the presidential contest.

United Democratic Forum’s Mudavadi, who had been expected to join ANC and at one time was presented as a compromise candidate and even signed a pact with Uhuru, who like Ruto was facing charges at the International Criminal Court (ICC), but which he quickly abandoned, in the end, went solo and emerged third in the contest.

Before then, we have the Kikuyu, Kamba, Kalenjin (KKK) Alliance that had onboard Uhuru, Kalonzo, and Ruto but which also was abandoned as soon as it was formed.

So, what guarantee is there that the One Kenya Alliance is not equally standing on quicksand and would not be swept away by emerging realignments as the 2022 election approaches.

Moi was quick to dismiss the notion, stating: “We did what skeptics thought we could not. We will do even more in the coming days.”

According to Wetang’ula, their mission is to unite all Kenyans, now that it is “clear that the county is keen on a new transformation agenda that not only unites Kenyans but also creates greater inclusivity.”

Knowing that it will need more than the joining forces of the four-party leaders to win power at the election, One Kenya Alliance has been quick to make it clear that it is willing to embrace other like-minded partners, including Raila.

But even as it forges ahead on this journey, the four leaders must be cognizant that the immediate threat that the alliance faces lies within.

This is because the bane of similar alliances in the past has been a failure by leaders to sacrifice their ambitions for the greater good.

In this regard, it remains to be seen whether Kalonzo, Muvadadi, Moi, and Wetang’ula will be willing to drop their dream of becoming President.

Of the four, only Moi and Wetang’ula do not see ascending to the top job as a do-or-die affair and might thus be willing to settle for other positions especially if the Building Bridges Initiative (BBI) constitutional changes are endorsed and more opportunities created in the national executive.

The main focus is likely to be on Kalonzo, who has previously made it clear that his time has come given the sacrifices he had made in the past, especially by agreeing to play second fiddle to Raila in 2013 and 2017.

At 65 years of age, Kalonzo, who first became MP in a 1985 by-election, is the eldest of the four and whether or not he will be willing to be bypassed yet again is the elephant in the room.

Whether or not the One Kenya Alliance makes it to the ballot next year, only time will tell.

Ugandan president Yoweri Museveni has reached out to a section of opposition coordinators and members allied to the National Unity Platform (NUP) party in a new political twist.

The meeting held at State House Entebbe on Thursday, May 6, aimed to discuss how the two parties can develop the East African nation.

TUKO.co.ke understands that NUP is a social-liberal and progressive political party led by Museveni's main challenger in the January 14 presidential elections, Robert Kyagulanyi Ssentamu, alias Bobi Wine.

"I appreciate their views on the different failures of the ruling National Resistance Movement (NRM), and my assurance is that we shall work together to address such," Museveni said in a tweet.

This comes a day after NUP leaders held talks with Justice Forum, commonly referred to as (JEEMA).

JEEMA was originally established as a pressure group in 1995 under the one-party system of NRM, as other political parties were not allowed until after the 2005 constitutional referendum.

NUP spokesperson Joel Ssenyonyi expressed confidence that the relationship between the two political outfits will grow stronger.

"The good thing with JEEMA is we are not starting a relationship. We have had it for a very long time, we are only strengthening it the way friends operate," Ssenyonyi told journalists.

However, Bobi Wine rubbished Museveni's claims that the group of people he held talks with was affiliated with NUP.

Bobi Wine accused Museveni, 76, of the continued torture and imprisonment of his supporters.

"Shameless! After killing, maiming, torturing, and imprisoning NUP supporters, he (Museveni) has the audacity to pay and parade people and claim they are NUP! His usual methods! He is too desperate to cleanse himself as the world gets to know the extent of his raw greed and brutality," Bobi Wine tweeted.

In February 2021, the Electoral Commission announced Museveni as the winner of the presidential elections with 58%. Bobi Wine came second with 35%.

Museveni is well-known for being accommodative by accepting to meet and listen to opposition politicians despite their divergent political opinion.
